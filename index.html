Explore AI: What is it, and where do we find it?

What is Artificial Intelligence (AI)?

Think of AI as computers or programs that can do things that usually require human intelligence. This can be learning from experience, solving problems, making decisions, or understanding language.

An important part of AI is Machine Learning (ML)Systems that learn from large amounts of data to find patterns and make decisions without being specifically programmed for each task.. For example, streaming services use ML to suggest movies you might like.

Large Language Models (LLMs)Advanced "computer brains" trained on enormous amounts of text and code. are a type of AI you might have heard of, like ChatGPT. They learn patterns in language and can generate text, answer questions, and much more. Important: LLMs don't "think" like humans; they are good at predicting the next word based on patterns they've learned.

AI is not magic!

It's important to remember that AI, including LLMs, works by recognizing and copying patterns from its training data. They have no real understanding or consciousness. This is key to understanding both what AI can do, and what it *cannot* do.

AI in your daily life and future profession

AI is not just something for the future – it's already part of your everyday life! You encounter AI when using social media (personalized feed), email (spam filter), GPS apps (route optimization), and smart speakers.

AI in Norwegian Vocational Fields:

AI is becoming a bigger deal in many jobs. It's showing up everywhere, helping with things like doing tasks automatically, figuring out problems, making designs better, and giving people more personal experiences.

In most professions, AI is about enhancingMaking better or stronger. human skills, increasing efficiency and safety, not necessarily replacing people. It's important to see how AI can become a useful tool in your specific field!

Why is AI literacy important for you?

AI literacyThe ability to understand basic AI concepts, critically evaluate AI systems, and use AI tools effectively and ethically. is more than just a technical skill. It's a key competency for the future.

  • Future-proof your career: AI is changing the job market. With AI literacy, you are better equipped for changes.
  • Make informed choices: Understand how AI affects society, including ethics, privacy, and possible biasUnfair leaning towards or against something or someone, often based on pre-existing ideas instead of facts..
  • Become a better professional: AI literacy helps you think critically and learn throughout life, which is important in all professions.

Being able to assess the information you get from AI tools is especially important. In many professions, important decisions are made. If an AI tool provides incorrect or biased information, you must be able to ask questions and check the information, not just accept it.

Think Critically: Navigating an AI-Driven World

How AI Learns (and Gets it Wrong): Bias In, Bias Out

AI models learn by analyzing enormous amounts of data, often sourced from the internet. If this data contains biasUnfair leaning towards or against something or someone, often based on pre-existing ideas instead of facts., the AI will likely reflect and perhaps even amplifyTo make something stronger or louder, like turning up the volume. these biases.

Examples of AI bias that could affect you:

  • Wrong "Facts" Online (Even in Norwegian!): Imagine you're searching online for info – maybe about rules for something, or even a historical event. An AI-powered search engine might give you answers in perfect Norwegian and even say they're "Norwegian rules." But, because the AI was trained on way more data from, say, the USA, it might actually be giving you American rules or perspectives, just translated and dressed up as Norwegian. So you might end up learning or doing something completely wrong for Norway, even though the AI *sounded* like it knew what it was talking about for your country.
  • Getting Skipped for an Opportunity (And No One Knows Why): Let's say in a few years you apply for a summer job or a special program. The company uses AI to sort through applications. Your application might get skipped, and neither you nor the company fully understands why. It could be that the AI was poorly designed to promote "diversity" and ended up accidentally filtering *out* male applicants for certain roles, even if that wasn't the goal. Or it might be looking for tiny, weird patterns in CVs that it *thinks* predict success, but actually have nothing to do with how good you'd be, accidentally discriminating against you.
  • Social Media Misunderstandings & Shadow Bans: You post something online – a joke, an opinion, a comment. An AI moderator, trained mostly on English internet drama or American cultural issues, might misunderstand your Norwegian slang, sarcasm, or cultural reference. It could flag your normal comment as "problematic," delete it, or even temporarily ban your account ("shadow ban" you so fewer people see your stuff) because it wrongly thinks you're breaking rules, even when you're just talking like a typical Norwegian teen.

For your vocational field, a biased AI could lead to incorrect diagnoses, unfair assessments, or designs that don't suit everyone. It's important to remember that AI bias often mirrors existing societal inequalitiesUnfair differences in how groups of people are treated or have access to opportunities in society.. Therefore, human oversight and critical assessment are always needed.

AI "Hallucinations" & **Looks Real, But Isn't**: Spotting Convincing Fakes

Sometimes AI models can generate information that is incorrect, misleading, or completely nonsensical, but present it as fact. This is called AI hallucinationsWhen AI invents facts or information that isn't true..

Why does this happen? AI doesn't 'know' things like humans do. It's trained to predict the next most likely word (or pixel, or sound) based on the billions of examples it has seen. Think of it like a super-advanced autocomplete. If you ask it, 'Who will win the Ballon d'Or in 2025?', it doesn't know the future. But it knows which football players' names often appear near 'Ballon d'Or winner'. So, it might confidently name a famous current player. It *sounds* believable because the name is right, the context is right, but the AI is just making a highly probable guess, not stating a known fact. It can be totally wrong.

This isn't deliberate deception by the AI. It's just how it works: predicting patterns. If the patterns in its data are incomplete or lead to a 'likely' but false statement, it will still generate it.

For example, an AI might 'invent' historical events or scientific 'facts.' The problem is made worse because AI can make its output look very real and professional (what I, Daniel the teacher, like to call 'the look of expertise'). Perfect grammar and a confident tone can make false information seem true, even when it's completely made up.

Misinformation, Echo Chambers & Filter Bubbles

On the internet, false or inaccurate information can spread quickly. It's important to distinguish between:

  • MisinformationFalse info spread, often without the sender knowing it's false.
  • DisinformationFalse info created and spread intentionally to deceive or harm.

AI can amplify these problems. Algorithms on social media can create:

  • Echo ChambersEnvironments where you mostly see opinions you already agree with, and alternative views are rarely encountered.
  • Filter BubblesWhen algorithms personalize the content you see based on your past behavior, so you miss other perspectives, often without knowing it.

This is related to the Attention EconomyThe idea that your attention is a valuable resource that websites and apps compete for, often through advertising.. AI is good at finding and promoting content that captures attention, regardless of whether it's true or good for you.

The Dangers are Real: This isn't just about being wrong; it's serious. Believing and spreading false AI-generated information can have severe consequences. In your future jobs, decisions based on bad info could lead to dangerous mistakes, financial loss, or harm to people's reputations or even their safety. Imagine an electrician relying on faulty AI-generated wiring diagrams, or a healthcare assistant acting on incorrect AI medical advice. Online, it can lead to bullying, scams, and even influence important societal events like elections with false narratives. Being critical isn't just a skill; it's a responsibility to protect yourself and others.

AI as Helper: Use the Tools Smartly and Responsibly

The "You First" Principle: Your Brain, Then AI

When using AI for school, the most important principle is: You First! This means you should always start with your own thinking, research, and effort *before* using AI tools.

View AI as a helper that can supportTo assist or hold up., improveTo make better., or refineTo make small changes to improve something. your own work – not as something to replace your own thinking. If you let AI do all the work, you don't build your own "thinking muscles."

Once you've thought through a problem or task yourself, using AI becomes more targeted. You can ask better questions (prompts) and assess AI's answers more critically.

AI for Augmentation vs. Generation

There's a big difference between using AI to augmentTo add to or enhance something. your work and letting AI generateTo create or produce. work for you.

Generation

AI creates content or solutions almost entirely from scratch, often based on a simple command (prompt), without much prior effort from you.

Example: "Write an essay about..."

Augmentation

AI helps you, improves, or builds upon work you've already started or thought out. AI is a collaborator.

Example: "Here's a paragraph I wrote. Can you give feedback on clarity and grammar?"

In school and professional life, the focus should be on augmentation. AI can help you see new angles, improve what you've created, or automate simple parts of a task, so you can focus on the more demanding and creative parts.

Ethical Use: AI as a Learning Partner (with Norwegian Guidelines)

With the "You First" principle and your teacher's permission, AI can be a great learning partner. Here are some examples of ethical AI use:

  • Brainstorming (after your own effort): "I've thought about X, Y, Z for my project. Can you suggest some other angles?" (Try the "AI Project Idea Expander" above!)
  • Getting feedback on YOUR work: "Can you check this text I wrote for grammar mistakes?"
  • Explaining difficult topics (after your own effort): "I've read about Ohm's Law but struggle to understand. Can you explain it simply for an apprentice in electrical trades?"
  • Practicing skills: "Give me a scenario for a customer conversation where the customer is dissatisfied."

Important in Norway:

  • AI as support, not replacement: AI should support learning, not replace your own work and critical thinking.
  • Be transparent: Be clear about when and how you've used AI.
  • Check facts: Information from AI must always be checked against reliable sources. Do not cite AI as a source.
  • Privacy: Don't share sensitive personal information or copyrighted material with AI tools, especially those not approved for GDPR (privacy regulations). Consider using tools like **Sikt AI Chat**, which is adapted to Norwegian privacy laws.

Ethical use is about your intention and transparency. The goal must be to learn more and become better, not to avoid work.

Academic Integrity: What's NOT Okay?

Misusing AI can be cheating. Here are some clear "red lines":

  • Submitting AI-generated work (text, code, solutions) as your own. This is plagiarism.
  • Letting AI do most of the work for you on an assignment.
  • Using AI when the teacher has said it's not allowed (e.g., on tests).
  • Asking AI to write entire reports or large sections of text for you.

The consequences can be serious, from a poor grade to failing. AI should not replace your own learning, critical thinking, or creativity.

Your AI Future: Opportunities and Human Strengths

AI's Impact on Your Vocational Field

AI will affect most professions, but in different ways. Here are some examples:

  • Electrical Trades & Computer Technology: Smart power management, automatic fault detection in circuits, AI tools for coding and cybersecurity.
  • Healthcare, Childcare, and Youth Work: AI can help analyze X-rays or automate administrative tasks, but human care and empathy cannot be replaced.
  • Building and Construction: Drones with AI to monitor construction sites, AI to optimize designs for energy efficiency.

The point is often that AI enhancesImproves or makes better. human skills and changes job tasks, rather than replacing skilled workers. It will be important to be able to collaborate with AI systems.

AI & Society: Your Responsibility as a User and Citizen

Your Digital Footprint & Privacy with AI

Every time you use digital tools, including AI, you leave a digital footprintTraces of your online activity.. When using AI, you often share data – what you ask, the information you seek, or personal details.

Important points about privacy with AI:

  • Data Collection: Many AI tools collect user data to improve their models or for business purposes.
  • Privacy Policies: Read (summaries of) privacy policies to understand how your data is used.
  • Sensitive Information: Be careful about sharing very personal or confidential information with public AI tools.
  • GDPR & Norwegian Laws: Norway has strict privacy laws (GDPR). Consider using AI tools approved for Norwegian conditions, like **Sikt AI Chat**.

The Attention Economy and AI's Role

The Attention EconomyThe idea that your attention is valuable, and tech companies design products to capture it, often for advertising revenue. is about your attention being valuable. Tech companies often design products to capture and hold your focus as long as possible, often to make money from advertising.

AI is an engine in this:

  • Personalization: AI algorithms tailor content (feeds, recommendations, notifications) to be as engaging as possible for you.
  • Engagement Optimization: AI can identify and promote content that captures attention (e.g., emotional, sensational), regardless of quality or accuracy.

Disadvantages can include increased distraction, impact on mental health, and spread of misleading content. For you as a vocational student, it's important to protect your focus for learning and future work.

Ethical Dilemmas in AI: Who Decides?

As a professional, you might face ethical dilemmas where AI recommendations clash with your judgment or professional ethics. Let's look at a couple of scenarios:

Scenario 1: Healthcare Worker

An AI diagnostic tool (90% accurate) suggests a treatment plan. Your experience and knowledge of the patient make you unsure. The AI's suggestion is data-driven, but your intuition says something else.

Dilemma: Do you follow the AI, your own judgment, or seek advice? How do you weigh statistics against individual care?

Scenario 2: Construction

An AI-driven design software suggests a cheap material that meets minimum requirements, which the client likes. Your knowledge indicates that a more expensive alternative is much safer and more durable in the long run.

Dilemma: Do you argue for the more expensive but safer option (risking a dissatisfied client), or follow the AI's cost-effective proposal?

Such dilemmas often have no easy answers. They show how important human judgment, ethical reflection, and the ability to critically question AI are.

Your Voice Counts: Contribute to Ethical AI Development

You're not just a passive user of AI – you can help shape how AI is developed and used ethically!

  • Give feedback: When using AI tools, provide constructive criticism about performance, bias, or usability.
  • Promote ethical practices: In your future job, advocate for ethical AI principles and responsible use.
  • Be mindful of the data you create: The data you generate can be used to train future AI models.
  • Join the dialogue: Engage in discussions about AI, such as in student councils or professional forums.
